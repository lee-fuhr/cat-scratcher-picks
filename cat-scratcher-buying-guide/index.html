How to choose the right cat scratcher

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To pick the best cat scratcher in 2026, match the material and shape to your cat's preferred scratching style, vertical posts for full-body stretches, horizontal pads for cats that dig into carpets, and angled or curved designs for in-between postures. Prioritize sisal rope over carpet or cardboard for longevity, and place scratchers near napping areas or entryways where cats naturally want to mark territory. Skip flimsy, lightweight models that tip over; a solid base or wall mount is worth the extra cost.

What type of scratcher does my cat need?

Cats scratch for different reasons, stretching muscles, marking scent, and maintaining claw health. The posture they assume tells you what shape works best. A cat that rises on hind legs and digs into the arm of your sofa needs a tall vertical post (at least 30 inches so they can fully extend). A kitty that rakes the floor or a rug wants a horizontal cardboard or sisal mat. Many cats also enjoy angled or curved scratchers that let them switch between vertical and horizontal mid-scratch. Observe where your cat already scratches. If they target a specific chair or corner, place a scratcher of similar orientation nearby. Experts at the Cornell Feline Health Center note that cats often scratch shortly after waking, so putting a scratcher next to their favorite sleeping spot dramatically increases adoption. A single shape won’t suit every cat; offering two or three types lets you see which gets used.

Which materials last longest and are safest?

Sisal rope, especially thick, tightly wound natural sisal, is the gold standard for durability and texture. It sheds fibers but stands up to years of clawing and encourages cats to dig in. Many commercial posts use a thin sisal wrap that unravels quickly, so look for posts with a heavy-gauge rope or replaceable sisal sleeves. Cardboard scratchers are inexpensive and satisfying for shredders but produce a mess and need replacement every few months. Carpet-covered posts feel good to some cats but can encourage them to scratch your wall-to-wall carpeting. Avoid materials treated with harsh glues or synthetic fragrances. Rope dyed with questionable chemicals can irritate paws. For recycled cardboard, choose corrugated layers that are dense and glued with non-toxic starch. If you buy a upholstered post, stick to tight weave fabrics like microfiber that won’t snag claws. Particleboard bases should be heavy enough to keep the post from tipping; a wobbly scratcher will be abandoned.

How important is size and stability?

A scratching post that topples over is worse than no scratcher at all, it frightens the cat and teaches them to avoid it. For vertical posts, the base should be at least 18 to 20 inches square if carpeted, or a solid wood base with a broad footprint. Post height matters more than diameter: a cat needs to reach full extension, so 30 to 36 inches is ideal for most adult cats. Taller posts (up to 45 inches) suit large breeds like Maine Coons. Horizontal cardboard scratchers range from small single-wave pads to long, crescent-shaped loungers. A mat that’s too light will slide across the floor, look for one with a non-slip back or a weighted edge. For wall-mounted or over-door scratchers, ensure the hardware supports a cat’s full weight pulling downward. Owner reviews frequently praise models that include a replacement sisal sleeve and a heavy wooden base.

Where should I place a cat scratcher to guarantee use?

Location determines success more than any other factor. Cats scratch to mark territory where they spend time and where they want other animals (or humans) to notice. High-traffic zones, the living room, hallway, or near the front door, are prime spots. Avoid hiding the scratcher in a quiet corner; your cat will choose your sofa instead. For multi-cat households, place several scratchers around the home so each cat can claim one without conflict. Another smart tactic: put a vertical post right next to the furniture your cat already targets. For example, if they scratch the side of the couch, plant a sisal post an inch or two away. Over several weeks you can gradually shift the post to a more convenient spot once the habit is formed. Sprinkle catnip on new scratchers for the first week, but stop if your cat over-ingests it or becomes overstimulated.

How can I train my cat to use a scratcher instead of my furniture?

Reward-based training works far better than punishment. When your cat uses the scratcher, immediately offer a treat, praise, or a brief play session. If they scratch furniture, gently redirect them to the post, don’t yell or squirt water, which can create fear. Cover the furniture’s tempting spot with double-sided tape, aluminum foil, or a clear plastic carpet runner (nub side up) temporarily. Cats dislike sticky or crinkly textures. Some cats ignore new scratchers out of habit. Try rubbing the post with a used sock or a soft cloth to transfer scent marks. Others respond to a dangling toy attached to the top of the post. For horizontal mats, sprinkle a bit of dried catnip and drag their paws lightly across the surface. Consistency over two to three weeks usually breaks the furniture habit. If nothing works, switch to a different material or shape, your cat may simply dislike sisal and prefer corrugated cardboard.

Frequently asked questions

How often should I replace a cat scratcher?

Cardboard scratchers typically last one to three months before they become too frayed to be satisfying. Sisal rope posts can last one to three years depending on usage and rope quality; replace when the rope tears easily or the post wobbles. Replaceable sisal sleeves and cardboard refills are cost-effective options.

Should I get a carpet-covered or sisal-covered post?

Sisal is generally preferred because its rough texture mimics tree bark and satisfies the natural tearing urge. Carpet-covered posts can confuse cats into scratching your floor carpets. Sisal also tends to last longer and sheds less. Some cats like both; if your cat already scratches carpet, a sisal post may be a better redirect.

What is the best height for a cat scratching post?

Most experts recommend a post that allows your cat to stretch its full body length. For an average domestic cat, 30 to 36 inches tall is ideal. Taller breeds or cats that love to climb may prefer a post of 40 inches or more. The post should be stable enough not to tip when the cat leans its full weight against it.

My cat only scratches the furniture, not the post. What do I do?

Start by putting the post directly next to the furniture they already scratch. They may need to learn that this new object is for scratching. Use positive reinforcement, treats, catnip, and praise, every time they touch the post. Make the furniture less appealing with double-sided tape or a deterrent spray. Be patient; switching habits often takes a couple of weeks.

Are cardboard scratchers safe for cats?

Yes, most are made from recycled cardboard using non-toxic glues. The shredded cardboard pieces are generally not harmful if ingested in small amounts, but it’s wise to choose a brand that specifies non-toxic materials. Avoid any cardboard that has visible ink or strong adhesive odors. Some cats love the texture, while others leave it untouched.

How many scratchers does one cat need?

Behaviorists often suggest providing at least two to three scratchers of different types (vertical, horizontal, angled) placed in different rooms. This gives the cat choices and reduces competition in multi-cat homes. If you have a single cat that already uses a post consistently, one high-quality scratcher in a prime location may be enough.

In shortThe best cat scratcher is the one your cat actually uses. Start by matching the shape to your cat’s natural scratching posture, choose sisal over carpet for durability, and place the scratcher where your cat already spends time. With a little patience and a well-considered placement, you can protect your furniture and give your cat a satisfying outlet for its instincts.
