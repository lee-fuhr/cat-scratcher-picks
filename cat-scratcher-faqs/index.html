Cat scratcher questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A good cat scratcher meets your cat’s natural need to stretch, mark territory, and maintain claw health. The best choice depends on your cat’s preferred scratching angle, the materials that appeal to them, and where you place the scratcher in your home. This guide answers the most common questions so you can pick a scratcher your cat will actually use.

What are the different types of cat scratchers and which one is best for my cat?

Cat scratchers come in several basic forms: vertical posts, horizontal pads, angled ramps, and combo units with perches or tunnels. Vertical posts, often covered in sisal rope or carpet, mimic tree trunks and are ideal for cats who like to stand on their hind legs and stretch upward. Horizontal cardboard or sisal mats suit cats that prefer to scratch low to the ground, such as seniors or kittens. Angled scratchers offer a compromise, and multi-level towers give your cat both climbing and scratching options. No single type is universally best; it depends on your cat’s habits. Watch how your cat naturally scratches furniture or carpet. If they claw the side of a sofa, a tall vertical post is likely the right match. If they dig at rugs, a flat cardboard or sisal mat works better. Many cats enjoy having both styles available, especially if you can place them near the areas they already target.

Why do cats scratch furniture and how can I redirect them?

Cats scratch for several overlapping reasons: to condition their claws by removing outer sheaths, to stretch their back and shoulder muscles, and to leave both a visual mark and a scent from glands in their paws. It is a normal, instinctive behavior, not a sign of spite. Understanding that scratching is essential to your cat’s physical and emotional wellbeing helps you approach the “problem” with empathy. To redirect scratching away from your furniture, first cover the targeted spots with a deterrent like double-sided tape or a plastic furniture protector. Then place an attractive scratching alternative immediately next to the covered area. Over time, gradually move the scratcher to a more convenient location. Praise or treat your cat when they use the approved surface. Consistency and patience matter more than punishment, which usually backfires by making the cat anxious.

What materials should I look for in a cat scratcher?

The most common and effective scratching materials are sisal rope, sisal fabric, corrugated cardboard, and carpet. Sisal rope is a natural, rough fiber that frays in a way cats love, it offers good resistance for a satisfying scratch. Sisal fabric is similar but laid flat. Corrugated cardboard is lightweight, inexpensive, and easy to replace; many cats enjoy the texture and the sound it makes. Carpet is less ideal because it can train your cat to scratch similar textures on your rugs or upholstery, but it is still better than nothing. Avoid scratchers covered in soft plush fabrics or synthetic materials that don’t catch claws well. The key is a surface that allows your cat’s claws to sink in slightly and then pull, removing the outer sheath. Sisal and cardboard outperform most other options in owner reviews and expert tests. Check the durability: a well-built post should have tightly wound sisal that won’t unravel within a month.

How many scratching posts does my cat need?

Most experts recommend at least two scratching surfaces per cat, placed in different areas of the home. One scratcher should be near your cat’s sleeping or resting spot, and another near a high-traffic area where they like to announce their territory, often near the entrance to a room or in the living room. For multi-cat households, provide one scratcher per cat plus one extra to reduce competition and territory disputes. The total number depends on your home layout and your cat’s personality. If your cat marks a particular spot (say, the corner of the couch), put a scratcher right there even if it means having three or four around the house. Cats are more likely to use a scratcher that is conveniently located and aligns with their preferred scratching angle.

Where should I place a cat scratcher in my home?

Placement is at least as important as the scratcher itself. Put the scratcher in a spot your cat already frequents or where they have already scratched. Common successful locations include next to the sofa, near a favorite window perch, in the hallway that leads to the litter box, or at the foot of the bed. Cats often scratch when they wake up from a nap, so a scratcher near a sleeping area is a smart bet. Avoid hidden corners or out-of-the-way rooms; cats scratch to make their presence known, so they prefer visible, central locations. If a scratcher is ignored for more than a week, move it gradually, a few inches each day, toward a more used area. You can also rub a bit of catnip on the surface or dangle a toy from the top to spark curiosity.

How can I encourage my cat to use a new scratching post?

Start by making the new post more appealing than the old target. Place it directly in front of the damaged furniture or rug, and cover the old spot with something unappealing like foil or double-sided tape. Lightly rub dried catnip into the sisal or cardboard, most cats respond well, though about a third won’t react. Gently move your cat’s paws over the surface in a scratching motion while they are already stretching, and reward any interest with a treat or play session. Never force your cat’s paws onto the post; this can create a negative association. Instead, use positive reinforcement and patience. Some owners find that leaning a scratching pad against the furniture at the same angle helps the cat discover the new texture. Over a few days to two weeks, most cats will adopt the post if the location and material match their preferences.

Frequently asked questions

How long do cat scratchers typically last?

It varies widely by material and how vigorously your cat scratches. Corrugated cardboard scratchers usually last two to six months before needing replacement. Sisal-wrapped posts often hold up for a year or longer, but heavy scratchers may fray sooner. A well-made, tall post can last several years if you flip or rotate it to spread out the wear.

Should I get a horizontal or vertical scratching surface?

Choose based on your cat’s preferred scratching posture. If your cat stretches up tall and claws the side of the couch, get a vertical post. If they crouch and scratch the carpet or rug, a horizontal pad is a better fit. Cats that like both angles may benefit from having one of each available.

Are cardboard scratchers better than sisal rope?

Neither is universally better, both have loyal fans. Cardboard is very budget-friendly, lightweight, and easy to replace, and many cats adore the texture. Sisal rope is more durable, supports vertical scratching that exercises different muscle groups, and often appeals to cats who like to stretch. Many owners find that offering both options keeps their cat satisfied.

My cat ignores the scratching post; what should I do?

First, check the location, move the post to a spot your cat already scratches or spends time. Next, try changing the material or angle; a cat that ignores sisal may love cardboard, or vice versa. Rub a small amount of catnip on the post, and use play sessions near it to create positive associations. Be patient; it can take a week or two for a cat to accept a new surface.

Can I use catnip to attract my cat to a scratcher?

Yes, catnip is a useful tool, but it doesn’t work on all cats, roughly two-thirds of adult cats respond to it. Rub dried catnip into the sisal or place a catnip-filled toy near the base. Avoid overloading the surface, as too much can overwhelm some cats. For non-responders, try silver vine or valerian root, which have similar effects for many cats.

Is it okay to trim my cat’s nails to reduce scratching damage?

Regular nail trimming is safe and recommended, it keeps claws blunt and reduces the damage from inevitable scratching. Use cat-specific clippers and trim just the tip to avoid the quick. Combined with good scratchers, trimming significantly lessens harm to furniture. You can ask your vet or a groomer to show you the technique if you are unsure.

In shortChoosing the right scratcher isn’t about finding one perfect product; it’s about understanding your cat’s instincts and habits. Offer a few different materials and placements, be patient with the transition, and remember that scratching is a healthy behavior you’re redirecting, not stopping. A well-chosen and well-placed scratcher saves your furniture and keeps your cat happy and thriving.
